‚öñÔ∏è Balance Rigor with Feasibility

Avoid measurement theater: Don't create elaborate M&E systems that look impressive but are impossible to implement. Good measurement is:

  • Actionable: You'll actually use the data to make decisions
  • Feasible: You have capacity and resources to collect it reliably
  • Essential: It answers questions you genuinely need answered
  • Ethical: Benefits to learning outweigh burden on participants

Default to measuring less, better. Five indicators tracked reliably beat twenty tracked poorly.

Understanding Types of Indicators

Different indicators measure different things. Choose the right type for what you want to learn.

‚öôÔ∏è
Process Indicators
Measure what activities you're doing and how well you're doing them. Track implementation quality and fidelity.
Example: "% of scheduled distributions completed on time"
üì¶
Output Indicators
Measure what you directly produce or deliver. Count beneficiaries reached, services provided, or goods distributed.
Example: "Number of children receiving supplementary food"
üéØ
Outcome Indicators
Measure short to medium-term changes in beneficiaries' behavior, knowledge, or status resulting from your program.
Example: "% of mothers exclusively breastfeeding to 6 months"
üåü
Impact Indicators
Measure long-term, fundamental changes in well-being. Often contributed to by multiple programs, hard to attribute.
Example: "Under-5 stunting rate in target communities"

üéØ Which Type Should You Focus On?

Most programs should prioritize output and outcome indicators.

  • Outputs are easiest to measure and prove you're doing what you said you'd do
  • Outcomes show whether your program is making a difference
  • Process indicators help you improve implementation quality
  • Impact is hardest to measure and attribute; often requires evaluation studies rather than routine monitoring

üí° Using Your Data Well

Data is only useful if you act on it. Schedule regular review sessions where you:

  1. Look at trends over time, not just single data points
  2. Ask "why?" when numbers are surprising or concerning
  3. Make concrete decisions based on what you learn
  4. Adjust your program when data shows something isn't working
  5. Share findings with staff and beneficiaries transparently
